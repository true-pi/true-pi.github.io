How did it all go wrong with our Pi ?

August 19, 2019

It all begins well over 2000 years ago (if you can believe history textbooks) in ancient Greece, with a man named Archimedes.

He devised a geometrical method of finding approximate value of Pi, by calculating perimeters of regular n-sided polygons.

The idea was basically this:

  • put one n-sided polygon that just fits inside the circle (inscribed polygon), and put another polygon that circumscribes the circle (fits around it).
  • The circumference of the circle will be somewhere between perimeter of inner and outer polygons.

Starting with two 6-sided regular polygons (hexagons), and doubling the number of sides in several steps, he reached two 96-sided regular polygons that more closely fit the curvature of the circle.

Result was a proud declaration that “proved” value of Pi to be:

223⁄71 < Pi < 22⁄7 ( 3.14085 < Pi < 3.14286 )

Unfortunately for our man Archimedes, and later, for thousands of mathematicians who proceeded to calculate this fake Foo Pi value to increasing number of decimal places … this proof and subsequent conclusion are simply wrong.

Where was the error, you may ask ?

It was in the assumption.

And assumption, as it is often said, is the mother of all … (you probably know that one).

Circle with two 6-sided regular polygons

Let us look at it in some detail, starting with basic 6-sided polygon (hexagon) in the picture.

Please forgive the ridiculous coloring, it is just for better visibility.

There is red-painted hexagon outside, blue circle, and green hexagon inscribed inside the circle. Note that we are measuring perimeters (length of lines and not surface area) of circle and polygons.

We have 6 equal segments, so everything we observe for one is valid for all of them. Let us look closely at one of these segments.

Assumption is:

x < a < y (for any value of n)

Where:

  • x - side length of inscribed polygon
  • a - arc length of a single segment
  • y - side length of circumscribed polygon
  • n - number of sides of regular polygon (n-sided regular polygon)

x < a is always valid - due to the obvious fact that straight line between two points is always shorter than any curved line going through these same two points. Straight line is the shortest path.

a < y (for any value of n) is not always correct, and this is the part that fails.

This part has never been mathematically proven, nor is it at all obvious from the picture.

a < y is only valid for regular polygons with relatively small number of sides (more precisely, n < 59). This may be the reason why it was overlooked in the first place, since it would take some skill to precisely create and measure polygons with larger number of sides.

As the n (number of sides) increases, y will tend to be closer and closer to x in length, and arc a will be longer than any of them by a small amount. Multiply that small amount by a number of sides n, and there you have the error.

If only our man Archimedes stopped at n = 48 , and did not do one more doubling of sides to reach n = 96 … he might had a correct conclusion, oh well.

Circle with two 96-sided regular polygons

Let us look more closely at same setup, but now with 96-sided polygons (same colors as above).

What, you see only green circle with some blur on the edges ?

That is correct, the difference between circles and polygons is so small on this scale, that only only green inner polygon is seen, and it looks like a circle at low resolution. (Better resolution pictures available below.)

Now, let us enlarge one of these 96 segments, for your viewing pleasure:

(For a better view, please open above image separately, by clicking here )

Question: which outline looks longest to you ?

If your answer was: arc of the blue circle looks longer than straight sides of either inner or outer polygon, you would indeed be correct. It is longer.

And that is why Pi cannot be calculated correctly using this method.

And all subsequent methods of determining Foo Pi, come from the same wrong assumption.

Conclusion

This is important to understand :

Length of a curved line cannot be correctly calculated using small segments of straight lines (rectification of a curve), no matter how small these segments are. Such method is only an approximation, and inherently contains an error.

Unfortunately this imprecise methodology is not exclusive to this case, but is a standard method on how science calculates dimensions of objects - rectification of the curve, Integral calculus.
