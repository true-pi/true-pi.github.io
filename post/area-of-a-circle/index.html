Area of a Circle - an interesting twist

August 25, 2019 - 5 minutes read - 949 words

How does area contained by the circle relate to True Pi value ?
Textbook equation for the area of a circle is:

Acircle = r2 π

where: r - radius of the circle ( r = d / 2 ),

but how true is this equation?

There are several types of textbook proofs, from geometric ones to ones using integral calculus (more details on Wikipedia page Area of a circle ).
We will take a look at “Rearrangement proof” that is described on that page, as this one is quite visual.

On a side note: we are still dealing here with Euclidean space, and this one can be well represented using Cartesian coordinates … which basically means straight lines perpendicular to each other from here to infinity. We can not assume that our space (universe) is anything like that. Please be aware of that.

Rearrangement proof - analysis

Let us start with circle and 6-sided regular polygons, inscribed and circumscribed.

We divide outer polygon into triangles, and rearrange them to create a very neat rectangle having following configuration:

Where:

  • r - radius of the circle ( r = d / 2 )
  • n - number of sides of regular polygon (n=6 in this example)
  • s - side length of outer regular polygon

As number of sides (triangles) n increases, circle will better fill-out polygons and we will get closer and closer approximation for the area of the blue circle.
Textbook claims that horizontal side of the rectangle (as we increase n) will tend to approach r π. Thus making area of the circle equal to that of the rearranged rectangle:

A = r rπ = r2 π

Now let us look more closely:

  • the vertical side is r, and will always be r - so no problems there
  • for the horizontal side of rectangle, it will certainly not approach half of the circumference of the circle as we increase n

In previous article we already established that circumference of the circle can not be correctly calculated by straight line approximation, since this approach contains inherent error. Circumference calculated in such a way is always shorter than actual value.
Horizontal side, therefore will never be equal to half circumference of the circle, it will be less.

What we can agree on is that A is proportional to r2 (this has been known for a long time, even before time of Archimedes ).

Therefore we can say:

Acircle = r2 SomeConstant    ( where SomeConstant < True Pi )

Let us now attempt to find out that constant (ratio of the area of a circle to the square of the radius).

Area of a circle - Archimedes to the rescue

We shall again return to our man Archimedes, and use his approach (already discussed previously ).
With one notable exception: instead of dealing with lines (perimeter), we are dealing with areas.

Picture shows one red outer polygon, blue circle on top, and inner green polygon on top of circle.

Looking at the picture, we can see:

AinnerPolygon < Acircle < AouterPolygon   , for any value of n >2

This is correct for all n, since outer red polygon always completely contains the circle … and therefore has the area that is always greater than the area of the circle. Also inner green polygon is always fully inside the circle.
When we increase n, areas of polygons and circle will get closer to one another (better fit), and we will be able to find approximation for the area of the circle, and subsequently SomeConstant (ratio of the area of a circle to the square of the radius).

Each polygon contains n-segments, and single segment is isosceles triangle (means triangle with two equal sides).
Area of a triangle is height (h) multiplied by half base (b/2):

Atriangle = h b / 2

We just need to calculate area of one triangle and multiply it by n to get area of the polygon. Since we have chosen radius of 1, then:

Acircle = SomeConstant

meaning:

AinnerPolygon < SomeConstant < AouterPolygon

Instead of using difficult calculations like Archimedes, let’s simplify by using some basic trigonometry on a unit circle (circle with radius r=1).
You may also check out Introduction to Trigonometry , in case you are not familiar with it.

We can easily determine that angle α inside polygon will be:
α = 360°/2n = 180°/n

Therefore areas of inner and outer polygon:

  • AinnerPolygon = n sin(180°/n) cos(180°/n)
  • AouterPolygon = n tan(180°/n) 1

We can see a trend, and our SomeConstant (ratio of the area of a circle to the square of the radius) seems to be none other than our good friend Foo Pi.
And therefore:

Acircle = r2 FooPi

What a nice surprise.

In Conclusion

Pi (True Pi) is defined as circumference divided by diameter of a circle.
Constant used to calculate area of a circle is different, and is not True Pi.
It is another constant, an old textbook friend, that we call Foo Pi, but maybe we can call it Other Pi or … whatever.

Textbook equation for area of a circle is therefore correct, and uses correct value (ratio of area to radius squared), even though incorrect assumption is used.

You may have heard that Two wrongs do not make a right, but in this case, it seems they do.

Until we meet again.
